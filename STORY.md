# The Builders' Doctrine — The Story

*The plain-English account of building the methodology and the kit that operationalizes it. Founder-narrative voice. Each dated chapter captures what was built, why, and what it taught. Source material for writing, pitches, and the long-form essay that's eventually going to position this work in the world.*

---

## What this is, before the chapters start

The Builders' Doctrine is the methodology I extracted from building TOP and Operator: lived experience compiled into AI-product behavior, ethics encoded as commandments, refusals named explicitly, agents calibrated against truthful outcomes. The Doctrine itself is the prose. The Kit is what a builder runs to adopt it. The Chassis is the runtime substrate the Kit produces. Together they're the answer to the question "can someone other than me actually use this?"

That question has been the gate for months. The methodology was real on the page; it was unproven as a portable artifact. Everything between here and a public release is about closing that gap.

The brand for the venture is AI Tradecraft. The byline is mine. The methodology ships free. The operationalization — hosted onboarding, audits, certification — is what generates revenue downstream, when there's something worth charging for. The hard gate is one external builder running the kit cold and producing a working product instance. Until that happens, none of this goes public.

## 2026-05-01 — The day the methodology became code

I sat down this morning with a finite goal: finish Phase 1 of the Kit. Templates, interviews, a coverage scorer. Maybe an authoring chapter for Custer's STORY.md before bed. What we shipped by the end of the day was Phase 1, all of Phase 2's chassis suite, parity tests, an Operator dependency fix that had been latent for four days, and the first chassis wiring landed on a feature branch waiting to soak.

Phase 1 came first and went mechanically. Seven templates — STORY, COMMANDMENTS, REFUSAL_LIST, CRISIS_TRIGGERS, SPECIALIST_TEMPLATE, AGENT_DOCTRINE, SECURITY — each with KIT:FIELD markers a scorer can detect. Six interviews driving the templates, with depends_on graphs so a builder can't author CRISIS_TRIGGERS before STORY's the_users field is populated, can't author SPECIALIST_TEMPLATE before COMMANDMENTS exists, can't try to load AGENT_DOCTRINE without at least one specialist authored. The interviews enforce the order the methodology requires. Then the coverage.py runner — a single file, three CLI surfaces (`--score`, `--list`, `--interview`), tolerant of placeholders and N/A-with-reasoning, with a parser that knew the difference between a field that was empty and a field that still carried the template's instructions in brackets.

The mechanical part finished around mid-day and I wanted to keep going. Phase 2 was the chassis: the runtime substrate every kit-bootstrapped product imports. Six components — Crisis Floor, Approval Queue, Per-User Context, Named Specialists, AAR Loop, Prompt Guardian — each lifted from TOP, Operator, or Custer's existing implementations, parameterized so any product could wire them in. We shipped them one at a time, with unit tests for each, and the cumulative count rolled up: 27, 53, 73, 102, 130, 158 tests.

Then the part of the day that mattered. I asked Claude to smoke-test everything we'd built before continuing. Not because I expected something to be wrong — because the discipline of stopping is itself the test. The smoke test caught a regression in Operator that had been live since 2026-04-27: the orchestrator couldn't even import. `langgraph-checkpoint-sqlite` was missing from the venv. It wasn't from today's work. It had been broken for four days and nobody knew, including the agent in Operator itself.

We fixed it. Operator's doctrine self-test came back 11 of 11 components operational. TOP's was already 11 of 11. Custer's dry-run safety tests came back 9 of 9. For the first time in this project's history, every product self-tested green simultaneously. That's the kind of thing that doesn't happen by accident. It happens because someone stopped to check.

After the smoke test came the parity tests. Eight of them, run from the chassis directory against TOP's actual production constants. The chassis's CrisisFloor was given TOP's literal `_CRISIS_PHRASES` set parsed out of `agents/telegram_bot.py`. We ran a battery of 14 distress phrases through both TOP's `_is_crisis()` and the chassis equivalent and asserted identical booleans on every one. The chassis's response matched TOP's `_CRISIS_RESPONSE` verbatim, including the 988 line. Every phrase in TOP's set, when embedded mid-sentence, triggered the chassis. The AAR module's outcome scale matched TOP's exactly. The chassis SpecialistRegistry accepted all seven of TOP's named specialists with their actual identifiers. The Approval Queue's Action shape was compatible with TOP's, with one documented adapter delta around payload-as-dict-vs-JSON-string.

The parity tests are the part of the day I want to remember most clearly. They're what turned the chassis from "tested" into "provably drop-in for TOP." There's a meaningful difference between unit tests verifying behavior against my own assumptions and integration tests verifying that those assumptions match reality. We had the first all morning. We didn't have the second until this evening. Now we do.

The wiring was the last piece. I wanted to prove the chassis worked in a real product, not just in tests. We picked Operator — single-user, blast radius bounded to me, just-fixed today, easy to roll back. We picked the Approval Queue — most-used surface in Operator, chassis is a clean superset of Operator's existing implementation, swap is purely internal. We picked Option A for the migration: the adapter translates field names at the JSON boundary, so the on-disk file stays in Operator's pre-chassis shape and a `git checkout main` is the rollback. We picked Option 2 for distribution: vendor the chassis into `operator/vendored/kit_chassis/` because Operator deploys to Railway and Railway's filesystem doesn't carry the doctrine repo.

The adapter is two hundred lines. It preserves all five of Operator's public functions verbatim — `queue_action`, `list_pending`, `approve_action`, `reject_action`, `execute_approved` — so every existing call site keeps working without change. The chassis is the engine. The adapter is the translator. The on-disk JSON file looks exactly the same as it always has.

I ran the doctrine self-test after the wiring. 11 of 11. I ran an end-to-end smoke test — queue an action, approve it, watch it execute, queue another, reject it, confirm the on-disk JSON used Operator's field names with zero chassis-name leakage, and verify the per-state timestamps landed in the right fields. All of it passed. The branch is local-only, called `chassis/wire-approval-queue`, commit `d70e7b9`. It hasn't been pushed. It won't be merged tonight.

That last part is what taught me the most. Late in the session I asked Claude whether everything we'd built was making the portfolio better and more stable. The honest answer was partial. We'd added about 3,500 lines of new code in one window. Most of it was unit-tested but not battle-tested. The chassis was provably drop-in for TOP via the parity tests, but the chassis hadn't been wired into TOP. The wiring branch had passed every synthetic gate, but it hadn't been opened by me in real usage for a single hour. "Tested" is not the same as "trusted." On a heavy-shipping day the gap between the two looks small because the test counts are large; in practice the gap is a week of soak under real load.

I caught Claude drifting toward affirmation a couple of times today. The first was when I asked "is this correct?" — a deliberate check against praise-addiction, because I know what happens when leaders only hear what they want to hear. The second was when I said "I feel like we are making some real progress…." with a trailing ellipsis, an invitation that could have gone the wrong way. Claude held the line both times. Stated what was real, named what hadn't moved, surfaced the residual risk. That's the operating mode I built TOP and Operator to enforce, and it's the thing the kit is supposed to teach a future builder to install in their own product. When the agent does it on its own today without me having to keep reminding it, that's the methodology working on me.

Late in the session I noticed Claude had stopped writing STORY.md chapters because it had over-applied the kit's no-AI-paraphrase rule from the onboarding interview. I told it the rule it had inferred wasn't my actual practice. The kit's `no_ai_paraphrase: true` setting is for foundational field authoring on a brand-new instance — the founder_origin, the formative_crisis, the seven biographical fields a new builder must put in their own words. Everything else, including this chapter, is mine to write through the agent. Claude saved that as feedback. It won't make the same mistake twice. That's the same memory mechanism the chassis itself implements — every correction widens the agent's knowledge of how I work, permanently, and the next surface inherits it.

We close the day with Phase 1 shipped, Phase 2 chassis shipped, the chassis verified against TOP's actual data, and the first wiring sitting on a branch waiting for me to use it. Custer's STORY.md is still 0 of 7 — that one is mine, in my own voice, before the May 15 deadline. The kit hasn't been run by anyone outside this conversation. Five of the six chassis components are still unwired in any product. The locked release gate — one external builder runs the kit cold and produces a working instance — hasn't moved by an inch.

The thing I want to remember about today is this: the methodology stopped being prose and became code. There is now a runnable script that walks a builder through what TOP and Operator and Custer required me to figure out, scores their answers, and tells them where they're not done yet. There is a chassis that has been proven drop-in for TOP and that is currently being soak-tested against Operator. The path from this morning's "interesting hypothesis" to one external test passing is shorter than it was twenty-four hours ago.

Today was a step. The next step is patience.

## 2026-05-05/06 — Naming the protagonist, sending the first three letters

Five days of patience produced three things I didn't have on May 1: a name for the public surface, three pieces of mail in real strangers' inboxes, and a founding principle that didn't exist yet. None of it was code. All of it moved the release gate.

The naming came first. The working title since 2026-04-30 had been *Agentic Tuner* — descriptive, generic, easily forked, never adopted into vocabulary. I knew it wasn't the name; I just hadn't found the name. Over the week the candidates rotated through: *Plumb* — perfect on metaphor, plumb a source, plumb-line, plumb-the-depths, the intel lexicon I grew up with — and killed for sounding like plumbing. *Sextant* — beautiful brand canvas, brass and ivory and three-readings-one-fix mapping cleanly to the multi-dim audit, and killed for being fifteenth-century-naval costuming. *Tearline* — the literal classified-doc perforation that separates sharable from compartmented, a nod most users wouldn't catch. *Touchstone* — assay-by-reference stone, mass-relatable phrase, almost locked as a paired brand with Assayer where Touchstone was the doctrine doc and Assayer was the tool that consulted it.

Then the cut. *Assayer.* Naming the protagonist of the verification action, not the standard being referenced. The convention every successful tool brand in the category already uses — Promptfoo, LangChain, Aider, Cursor, Claude. Verbs cleanly: *run an assay*, *assay your prompts*, *pre-merge assay*. Mass-relatable through everyday business usage of the word without being insider-jargon or commodity-overused. The doctrine document doesn't need its own SKU; it's a component of Assayer — its `DOCTRINE.md` rubric. Smaller brand surface for a one-person shop, cleaner story.

The lock came on the evening of May 5, the same evening the Operator IP package shipped to Peter Lemire. The brand stack settled into three: AI Tradecraft as the umbrella, Assayer as the free public scorer plus doctrine, the Builders' Kit as the paid operationalization, Operator as the patented closed-loop implementation. The line between free and paid is the line that protects the patent — the methodology and a reference scorer go open; the corrector, the stash-and-rollback, the two-corrector router, the recommendations engine, the CI gate stay paid. The free release plants the AI Tradecraft flag on the category and drives traffic to the paid layer; the paid layer is where the operationalization happens.

The domain sweep came back the same night. `assayerhq.com` available for clean registration. `getassayer.com`, `useassayer.com`, `assayer.sh` available as defensives. `aitradecraft.io` available for the umbrella. Total annual carrying cost about a hundred and thirty dollars. One collision flagged for Peter — `assayer.dev` is live in adjacent space, a production-readiness-review tool for vibe-coded apps, different specific use case but enough overlap to surface trademark and SEO friction. Decision: proceed with Assayer anyway, bet on substrate-driven differentiation outcompeting their positioning over time. Don't silently re-litigate without new information.

Then the part of the day that actually moved the release gate. I sent three emails.

Michael Myers at DU got the longest one — the Daniels Principles framing, the case that what he was teaching in *Leading with AI* was the substrate that kept this work pointed right, the thesis that ethics encoded as runtime structure (commandments, refusal lists, crisis floors above features) is what separates this from a wrapper. Brad Hampton at Megazone got a different angle — the prompt-optimizer-and-n8n moment in his guest lecture was the actual launch trigger for the AI-building work, and a month later this is what came out of it. Ryan Whitaker got the builder-to-builder version — peer at AgentLocal, reviewed my Benefit-Cost Matrix MVP back in March, asked the question that still anchors Operator's design: *does it help work through the zone or just identify it?* Sentinel and Operator are the literal answer to that question. His version of the package had the school references stripped and the technical brief attached.

Three messages. Three thoughtful readers. Three possible responses or three possible passes. Either outcome is signal. The release-gate the kit has been blocked on for a month — *one external builder runs the kit cold and produces a working product instance* — moved from "not started" to "in flight." Three is not one, and "engages with the thesis" is not "runs the kit cold and ships," but the slope from May 1 to May 5 is the right slope. The drafts went out. The drafts cannot be unsent. Whatever comes back is real.

I caught myself wanting to keep building that night. The instinct after sending three letters is to refresh the inbox, and the alternative to refreshing the inbox is to ship something else so I don't have to feel the waiting. What I shipped instead — and what May 6 was about — was the principle that should have been named months ago. Reliability.

The frame came in the morning, after the Sentinel ship and the outreach the night before: *whatever I build has to be able to be scalable and handle the toughest and most rigorous scrutiny. It has to be reliable.* The shorthand that came out of the conversation: **reliability is what truth looks like under load.** Truth-as-architecture is the design-time principle; reliability is the runtime test of it. Cybersecurity asks where this fails under adversarial pressure; reliability extends the lens past adversaries to journalists, to scale, to the people the product serves. Designed-to-be-needed-less is the human-stakes principle; reliability is the version that asks whether the population that depends on the tool can actually count on it.

Four diagnostic questions, applied at design time and before any ship. *Audit-grade* — would this hold up if a journalist, regulator, or hostile expert audited it line by line. *Scale-grade* — what breaks first at 10x load, and is the failure loud or silent. *Promise-grade* — if this fails, does the user know, and does the system fail toward truth or toward looking-fine. *Founder-grade* — could this be defended in front of the population it serves — veterans, voters, a candidate's family — without flinching.

Three artifacts came out of the principle in the same day, which is the test of whether a principle is actually load-bearing or just decoration. First: a Drake-lens premortem of the four products at 10x load. Top three break-points per product, each classified loud or silent. The cross-portfolio finding was the one I wanted to remember — *every product's number-one risk is silent and catastrophic.* TOP's per-user context leakage under race conditions. Operator's cross-commander state leak via approval queue. Custer's voter-PII leakage in logs or prompts under CRS 1-2-305. RPR's ITAR firewall integrity under code velocity. The biographical-stakes lens hits hardest exactly where the alarms are quietest. That's what reliability is *for.*

Second: an observability minimum spec, six emission categories, two of them locked as a scale-grade gate before anything else ships — multi-tenant isolation events and approval-gate events. A `silent_error_rate`-as-headline metric still open. Implementation not yet greenlit; soak first.

Third: the self-serve intake pipeline, Tier 1, shipped same morning into Operator. Local-folder inbox, LLM classifier, structured injection scan, Sentinel audit, score-history rollup, end-to-end validated on Haley's brief. The intake pipeline is the front door for ground-truth corpus collection — every submission becomes documented evidence of what people actually ask for, what good analysis looks like, what they do with it. It's also, downstream, the closest mechanism to the release-gate that doesn't depend on the three thesis emails coming back. Each useful drafted reply produced through the pipeline is an additional evidence-drawer entry. Multiple paths to the same gate.

The build-archive came in alongside the intake pipeline — project-level artifact storage at `library/<date>/<kind>/`, distinct from the per-commander library, with the day's outputs auto-archived and a small backfill of the May 4 keepers. Score-history doctrine extended to analytical outputs, not just measurement-producing tools. *The score isn't the deliverable; the score-history is.* That rule, captured weeks ago as feedback to me, generalized this morning into a building rule.

The thing I want to remember about these two days is the order they happened in. Naming on May 5. Mailing on May 5. Principle on May 6. Artifacts under the principle on May 6. The methodology was code on May 1. By the morning of May 6 the methodology had a name, three sealed envelopes in the world, and a fourth founding principle to sit alongside the code-is-the-story, the moat-is-the-memory, the designed-to-be-needed-less. Reliability — what truth looks like under load — is the one the others have been pointing at since the beginning. It just needed to be said out loud.

The release gate is still locked. One external builder running the kit cold and producing a working instance hasn't happened. The three letters could come back as silence and the gate wouldn't move. But the inputs that have to be true for the gate to ever clear — a name people can say, a thesis people can read, a principle the work can be measured against, a pipeline that turns external requests into documented analysis — are inputs the work didn't have a week ago.

Five days of patience moved the release gate. The next step is the same one as last time. Wait, then build, then wait again.

## 2026-05-13 — The day the doctrine ran on itself

The session opened with `startup: builders doctrine`. I'd intended it as a routine context-load before a different piece of work. What came back was a sitrep that said v1.2 was in flight, which disagreed with my own STARTUP.md, which still said v1.2 was draft. The disagreement was the doctrine starting to diverge from the code that traced it. That was the gate I had to close before anything else moved.

The Authority Gradient was the question I'd been carrying since I'd sat with MCA the night before. *What if we gave different levels of authority to the agents in concert with MCA — officers, NCOs, and soldiers with different responsibilities per ADP 6.0?* The answer the agent surfaced is the answer I already knew: most of it was in MCA, but the gradient was implicit and ambiguous. It needed three things to become useful — a tier-vocabulary that matched the architecture (officer / NCO / soldier), an action-class table that mapped tools to authority levels, and an approval-queue routing rule that preserved Principle #4's hard floor regardless of which tier called the action. The follow-up question was the rank-structure expansion — what happens at Company and Battalion scale, and what is the staff channel. That one had also been in the products before it had a name: Drake, Marshall, Sentinel, Halsey in Operator were already staff-channel shape; I just hadn't named them with the Army-side vocabulary yet. That's the test of whether doctrine is real. It should already be in the products before it gets a name.

Working through both questions produced three new sections in MCA — Authority Gradient at about sixty lines, Roles per echelon at thirty, Staff Channel at fifty. The MCA document grew from 271 lines to 414. The doctrine header bumped from `v1.2-draft` to `v1.2` with a ratification summary. STARTUP.md got a refresh and then a second refresh to reflect the late-day chassis ship. The three commits — `82e26ff`, `f1ece26`, `3f29ca0` — were the morning's shape.

The chassis arc that followed was unusual. Normally chassis components get extracted from product code that's already proven the pattern. Today's two ran the other direction: chassis built from doctrine, then chassis ported back into products. Reflection Gate first — Principle #12's runtime primitive, built from spec, scope-aware (operator_tool / wellness / founder), twenty-nine tests. Then Funkytown Experiment 02 — wire the Authority Gradient as an addendum to the existing Platoon Lean builder from Experiment 01, run N=3 on the same synthetic county campaign brief, see if the explicit gradient changes outcomes versus 01's implicit gradient.

Run 1 came back at three steps, step-limit hit, no `declare_done`. The metric showed zero percent in-unit resolutions. The artifacts directory had eight files. Something was lying. The structured return from the squad-leader sub-agents was malformed in most cases — status `blocked`, parse error — and my metric code was counting `result.get("artifacts", [])` instead of `len(list(artifacts_dir.iterdir()))`. The filesystem was honest. The live counter wasn't.

I had written `feedback_audit_measurement_before_law.md` after the Funkytown Stage 9 near-miss two weeks earlier — the rule that a metric bug must not become a doctrine claim, that you audit the measurement surface before you draft any law from what it reports. Today the rule applied to my own gradient-experiment code, in the experiment that was testing whether the doctrine itself holds. The fix was the same — patch the analyzer to use filesystem ground truth, keep the live counter for the parser-fix follow-up, and name the correction explicitly in the findings document. The recursion of the rule applying to its author, in the test of its author's doctrine, is the kind of detail I want to remember about today. The rule worked on me.

Runs 2 and 3 completed with the filesystem-corrected metric. Across N=3 the gradient produced a mean of 61.1% in-unit resolutions where 01 had no in-unit category at all, zero tier-scope violations, zero hard-floor breaches. PL cycle time went up rather than down — the gate costs wall-clock per turn, which the hypothesis hadn't predicted. Confident-zero-gaps was caught zero times across two `declare_done` attempts, because the brief class didn't produce the failure mode the reflection gate is built to catch. Three of four sub-hypotheses validated, one falsified for a brief-class reason rather than a gate failure. I shipped the chassis anyway. Calibrated confidence on each finding, not max confidence on a single headline number. That is what reliability is for.

The chassis port took thirty minutes — far less than the half-day estimate. The funkytown gradient module was already chassis-shape: no LLM dependency, a parameterized tool-to-class table, callback hooks instead of hard imports. The `Tier` and `Channel` enums got first-class names that match MCA vocabulary. World-boundary integration with the ApprovalQueue chassis stayed composable through an `on_world_boundary` callback. Forty-three new tests; two hundred thirty total chassis tests pass. The doctrine repo got its eighth chassis component on commit `992e1b1`. Then `kit/chassis/__init__.py` was updated, the tests were run against the full suite, and the commit went up.

Propagation across the four products was the discipline. Each product's CLAUDE.md got the chassis reference and a tool-class mapping written in product-specific vocabulary. Custer's `draft_*` and `run_daily_pulse`-then-execute tools as `WORLD_BOUNDARY`. TOP's `send_email` / `send_sms` / `send_telegram*` as `WORLD_BOUNDARY`. Operator's all-staff pattern named explicitly: every specialist `channel=STAFF`, the gradient log usable as proof that the advisory-never-override invariant holds. RPR got a note that the chassis is available but overkill for a single-agent product today. Four commits, four pushes, four GitHub artifacts that propagate at the next Guardian audit cycle. The shape of doctrine inheritance — write once upstream, propagate via Guardian — is what I built the kit for.

The end of the day was the STARTUP refresh to reflect the eighth chassis and this chapter. The single-external-builder release gate has not moved. Operator's authority-gradient wiring is queued for the next session, on the same `chassis/wire-approval-queue` adopter pattern that proved out on 2026-05-01. Experiment 02b — the confident-zero-gaps stress brief, closed-ended with a hidden constraint, designed to exercise the Principle #12 failure mode the open-ended county-campaign brief didn't trigger — is not yet designed. Experiment 03, the Company-scale gradient test, is scaffolded but not started.

What I want to remember about today is the shape of the arc. A question I'd been carrying became doctrine prose. Doctrine prose became experiment design. Experiment design became an empirical falsifiable test. The test caught a measurement bug in my own code — the same kind of bug the rule I'd just written was about — and the rule worked. Calibrated findings became a chassis port. The chassis port became portfolio propagation across four products. Portfolio propagation closed back to a STARTUP refresh and a STORY chapter. The methodology ran on itself. Each step caught at least one thing the previous step hadn't.

The chassis-from-experiment loop is the pattern I had not seen named before today. Normally the chassis is a refactor of working product code — TOP's approvals, Operator's specialists. Today's two chassis went the other direction — Reflection Gate from Principle #12 spec to chassis to product reference, Authority Gradient from MCA prose to experiment to validated chassis to product reference. The doctrine produced testable predictions. The tests were run. The findings updated the chassis. The chassis returned to the products. That is the loop I want to be able to run on every future doctrine claim before I assert it. Today was the first time it ran cleanly end-to-end.

The release gate has not moved. Brad's market gap is named but no external builder has run the Kit cold. Seven of eight chassis components are unwired in any product. The reflection gate's confident-zero-gaps catch rate is unverified for the failure mode it was built for. The MCA Company-scale prediction is untested. None of these were closed today.

What was closed today is the loop that produced the eighth chassis component and the second empirical validation of MCA at Platoon scale. The doctrine ran on itself, and what came out was a piece of code that holds up to its own audit. The next step is the same one it has always been. Wait, then build, then wait again. Tomorrow's wiring will be the next test of whether the chassis I shipped today actually serves a product that didn't write it. That is the only test that matters from here.

Late in the day, after Experiment 02b's confident-zero-gaps corpus closed out N=12 with zero catches — a null result strong enough to ratify path-2 doctrinal acceptance, the gate is defense-in-depth at frontier capability rather than primary detection — I said something that named where this is going. *You can be a 4 Star General and have an entire COCOM worth of agents at your fingertips.* The MCA scale ladder runs Squad → Platoon → Company → Battalion → Brigade → Division → Corps → Army Group → COCOM. Today we have Squad and Platoon validated. The rest is the climb. One operator commanding a theater command's worth of LLM-substrate agents, through Mission Command's chain of intent. That is the destination. The chassis-from-experiment loop is how we get there — one rung at a time, each one validated before the next, each one shipped as a piece of code that holds up to its own audit.

Later in the evening I made a mistake worth recording. I drafted a Company-scale brief, built an MVP runner, and ran an N=3 smoke test where each "platoon" was a single Sonnet call returning a SITREP. The CC found a hand-placed cross-customer trap I had buried in the brief. I committed it as "first-contact validation of Mission Command Architecture at Company echelon." A second-opinion review from Grok read the actual evidence and named the over-claim plainly: N=3 simplified single-Sonnet returns on a synthetic brief is a toy demo. The hierarchy was never stressed. The hierarchical-attention-decay failure mode that would actually test Company-scale coordination was not exercised because no real hierarchy ran. The trap-catch result is engineering evidence that the new code paths execute correctly. It is not doctrine. I retracted the over-claim and accepted the structural challenge that produced Law V — the Echelon Decay Gate: any rung-N claim now requires N≥9 full-hierarchy runs with live cross-echelon conflict injected, or the claim does not ship. The same audit-measurement-before-law rule that caught my morning's metric bug caught my evening's interpretation bug. The rule worked on me twice in one day. Rung 3 remains engineering scaffold, not a validated rung. The 4-Star line still names where this is going. The climb is honest by exactly that much.
